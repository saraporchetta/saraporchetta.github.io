Research

Our group studies the two-way interactions between the offshore environment and offshore wind energy. We investigate how atmospheric and oceanic conditions—including boundary-layer dynamics, waves, and air–sea interaction—affect wind farm performance, and how offshore wind farms, in turn, modify the surrounding atmosphere, ocean, waves, and marine environment across spatial and temporal scales.

We combine observations, high-resolution numerical modelling, mesoscale simulations, and theoretical analysis to study these coupled processes from individual wind farms to the regional North Sea. By improving their representation in weather, climate, and wind-energy models, we aim to reduce uncertainty, strengthen fundamental process understanding, and support the sustainable development of offshore renewable energy.

Research projects

NWO Veni Project
PRECIOS: PREcipitation Changes Induced by North Sea Offshore Wind Farms

The rapid expansion of offshore wind farms introduces new interactions between wind turbines and the atmosphere. While offshore wind farms are known to modify atmospheric flow and turbulence, their potential influence on cloud and precipitation processes remains insufficiently understood.

PRECIOS combines observations, high-resolution atmospheric simulations, and future offshore wind development scenarios to investigate how wind farms may influence precipitation in the North Sea region. The project examines the links between turbine-induced flow changes, cloud formation, rainfall, and their representation in weather and climate models.

By improving understanding of these processes, PRECIOS aims to support more reliable atmospheric modelling and provide useful knowledge for the scientific community, meteorological institutes, policymakers, and the offshore wind sector.

NWO – Dutch Research Agenda (NWA)
North Sea POWER: Understanding the ecological impacts of offshore wind farms

North Sea POWER is a multidisciplinary research programme investigating how offshore wind farms influence ecological processes in the North Sea. The project examines interactions between physical changes induced by wind farms and the responses of marine and aerial ecosystems, integrating expertise from atmospheric science, oceanography, ecology, and environmental modelling.

Our contribution focuses on understanding the atmospheric and physical processes that drive ecological change and on improving the representation of these coupled interactions across the atmosphere, ocean, and marine environment. The project aims to provide the scientific basis for balancing offshore renewable energy development with ecosystem protection and sustainable marine management.
